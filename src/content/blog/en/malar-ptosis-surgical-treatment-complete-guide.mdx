# Malar Ptosis: Complete Guide to Surgical Treatment for Natural Facial Rejuvenation

Malar ptosis, characterized by the sagging of tissues in the midface region, is a common complaint that accompanies the aging process. This tissue displacement not only contributes to a tired and sad appearance but also alters facial harmony and contour. Understanding the causes, recognizing the signs, and knowing the surgical treatment options are crucial steps for anyone seeking natural and long-lasting facial rejuvenation.

This complete guide, based on the Modern Face philosophy and Dr. Robério Brandão's experience, explores malar ptosis in detail, from its origins to the most modern surgical techniques to restore your face's beauty and vitality.

## What is Malar Ptosis and Why Does It Happen?

Malar ptosis, also known as midface sagging, is the result of weakening and volume loss of soft tissues that support the cheekbones.

[Image: Illustration showing malar region anatomy with and without ptosis]

### Multifactorial Causes of Malar Ptosis

Several factors contribute to the development of malar ptosis:

*   **Natural Aging:** Over the years, collagen and elastin production decreases, resulting in thinner and less elastic skin.
*   **Volume Loss:** Facial fat, which provides support and volume to cheekbones, tends to decrease and shift downward.
*   **Bone Resorption:** The facial bone structure also undergoes changes with aging, losing support and contributing to soft tissue sagging.
*   **Gravity Action:** Gravity exerts constant traction on facial tissues, accelerating the sagging process.
*   **Genetic and Environmental Factors:** Genetic predisposition and factors like sun exposure, smoking, and inadequate diet can also influence malar ptosis development.

### Recognizing Signs of Malar Ptosis

Malar ptosis manifests in several ways, affecting appearance and facial expression:

*   **Prominent Nasojugal Groove:** Tear troughs become deeper and more marked, creating a shadow under the eyes.
*   **Malar Fat Descent:** Cheekbones lose projection and volume, resulting in a "flattened" and undefined look.
*   **Nasolabial Fold Deepening:** The "smile lines" become more evident, accentuating the aging appearance.
*   **Loss of Jawline Definition:** Sagging of midface tissues contributes to loss of jawline definition, creating a "jowls" or "bulldog" appearance.

## Surgical Treatments for Malar Ptosis: Restoring Facial Harmony

Although fillers and other minimally invasive procedures may offer temporary improvements, surgical treatment is the most effective and long-lasting option to correct malar ptosis.

### Available Surgical Options

Several surgical techniques can be used to treat malar ptosis, each with its particularities and indications:

*   **Midface Lift:** This technique aims to lift and reposition midface tissues, restoring malar volume and smoothing grooves.
*   **Blepharoplasty:** In some cases, blepharoplasty (eyelid surgery) can be combined with a midface lift to improve the under-eye contour and smooth tear troughs.
*   **Canthopexy:** This procedure aims to strengthen and reposition the outer corner of the eye, improving lower eyelid support and correcting "droopy eye" appearance.
*   **Endomidface:** A minimally invasive technique utilizing small incisions and an endoscope to lift and reposition midface tissues.

## Preparation and Recovery for Malar Ptosis Surgery

### Pre-operative Preparation

Before undergoing any surgical procedure to treat malar ptosis, it is fundamental to follow your surgeon's guidelines:

*   **Pre-operative Exams:** Perform all requested exams to assess your general health and identify possible risks.
*   **Medications:** Inform about all medications in use, including supplements and herbal remedies, and follow guidelines on which to suspend before surgery.
*   **Habits:** Stop smoking and alcohol consumption at least two weeks before surgery to optimize healing and reduce complication risks.
*   **Fasting:** Follow guidelines regarding fasting period before surgery.

### Post-operative Recovery

Recovery after malar ptosis surgery varies according to technique used and individual patient characteristics. However, some general guidelines can be followed:

*   **Rest:** Avoid physical exertion and intense activities in first weeks after surgery.
*   **Medications:** Use medications prescribed by your surgeon to control pain, swelling, and infection risk.
*   **Incision Care:** Keep incision clean and dry, following guidelines on dressings and hygiene.
*   **Compresses:** Apply cold compresses to operated area to reduce swelling and discomfort.
*   **Position:** Sleep with head elevated to minimize swelling.
*   **Diet:** Maintain a healthy diet rich in nutrients to promote healing.
*   **Follow-up:** Attend all follow-up appointments so your surgeon can assess your recovery progress and provide additional guidelines.

### Risks and Complications

Like any surgical procedure, malar ptosis treatment presents some potential risks and complications, such as:

*   **Swelling and Bruising:** Common in first weeks after surgery and tend to decrease gradually.
*   **Sensitivity Changes:** Operated area may present sensitivity changes, such as numbness or tingling, which are generally temporary.
*   **Asymmetry:** In some cases, asymmetry between face sides may occur, which can be corrected with additional procedures.
*   **Infection:** Although rare, infection can occur and requires antibiotic treatment.
*   **Nerve Injury:** In extremely rare cases, facial nerve injury may occur, resulting in facial mimicry alterations.

By choosing a qualified plastic surgeon and following all pre and post-operative guidelines, it is possible to minimize complication risks and optimize your surgery results.

## Detailed Post-Operative Care

Procedure success depends as much on surgical technique as on post-operative care.

### First Days: Critical Phase

In this phase, small cares make big difference:

- **Elevated rest**: keep head elevated at 30-45° even when sleeping
- **Cold compresses**: apply in first 48h to reduce edema
- **Rigorous medication**: follow exactly prescribed schedules
- **Light diet**: prefer soft foods and avoid excessive chewing
- **Abundant hydration**: water helps in tissue recovery

### First Week: Intermediate Care

As healing advances:

- **Careful hygiene**: clean area as directed, without rubbing
- **Avoid sun exposure**: use hat and sunscreen when necessary to go out
- **Gentle facial movements**: avoid exaggerated expressions
- **Adequate sleep**: body recovers mainly during sleep
- **Avoid alcohol and tobacco**: both harm healing

### Weeks 2-4: Gradual Return

Return to activities should be progressive:

- **Light activities**: short walks are allowed
- **Remote work**: can be resumed as per comfort
- **Light makeup**: generally allowed after 2 weeks
- **Moderate exercises**: only with medical clearance
- **Lymphatic drainage**: indicated to accelerate recovery

### What to Avoid

During entire recovery period:

- Direct sun exposure on operated areas
- Intense physical activities before clearance
- Alcohol consumption in first 2 weeks
- Smoking (ideally stop 4 weeks before and after)
- Non-prescribed anti-inflammatories
- Sudden movements with face
